Details

Safe and Sound is the capstone project I completed for my Master's in Digital Product Design at Parsons School of Design.
Context
On January 15, 2022, Michelle Go, 40, of Manhattan, was pushed in front of an R train as it approached a 42nd Street platform one morning. The attack was random, and injected New Yorkers with grief and the collective fear that they might be the next victim.

In fact, in today’s world, it is 12x more likely that they are the next victim. With the onset of Covid-19, the number of violent subway felonies in New York City has spiked to 6 per million rides, which roughly equates to one victim every four hours.

Having moved to the city four months prior to the Go attack, I too was feeling anxious on my daily commute. This anxiety is ultimately what fuelled my investigation on how digital technologies might alleviate some of the fear felt while riding the subway, and overall make it a safer and more pleasant place to be.
Research
Phase
My investigation began by conducting a combination of primary, analogous, competitive, observational and user research, from which I extracted three meta insights:


Subway crime and ridership have an inverse relationship, as do subway crime and police presence ↑ Observational Research: Crime on the 42nd Street station platform.

To understand this, we first need to look at the history of the New York subway system. In the 90s, there were approximately 19 felonies/million rides. Comparatively, this number was down to 0.5 felonies/million rides in 2019. This was found to be because of an increase in subway-policing, and the fact that ridership had over doubled in that time, making it at a record-high.

And then 2020 happened. With the pandemic, the number of violent felonies spiked to 6 felonies/million rides because police resources were strained by Covid-19, so the enforcement of transit crimes fell to nearly zero. Compared to previous decades, the subway is still quite safe; but, the abrupt acceleration in crime is the biggest we’ve seen in history, so ridership is low.

Interestingly, even though crime goes down with increased ridership, most people that still do ride today, ride alone. Notably, most people are alone at the time of being a victim of subway crime.

Women’s fear of subway crime is more of a problem than crime itself ↑ Primary Research: Subway-related fatalities by gender, 1990-2003. (Source)

100% of the female interviewees from my user research voiced concerns about being pushed into the subway tracks. Interestingly, this fear isn’t justified by statistics which consistently show low rates of crime against women in subway spaces. Men actually constitute 84% of subway suicides, accidents, and homicides in the past decade; women, only 16%.

Probing a bit deeper, I wanted to understand why and what exactly women are scared of, and the most common theme I found was the unexpected.

Women are scared of the unpredictability and randomness of certain crimes and behaviors.

In my research, I found another explanation for this fear, and it’s that women have a greater propensity to think of past experiences of victimization in present situations. The irony is that women are more fearful of subway crimes than men, but men are more often victimized.

Women modify their behaviors in ways that make themselves feel mentally safer, but not necessarily physically safer on the subway system Again, 100% of my female interviewees reported modifying their behavior when on the subway, most notably by standing in the middle of the platform; sitting in the middle of the train so that they don’t get robbed as people exit; and not listening to music so as to remain alert.

What especially struck me interesting, was when one interviewee said she carries mace and a pocket knife for self defense and holds them to feel safer, but quote on quote said:

“I don’t even know what I’d do though. I’d probably injure myself before someone else. It’s more of a mental security thing.”

Comparatively, one of the answers from a male interviewee in response to: “how do you practice subway safety?”, was that he’s never thought of the subway as a safety risk, even though he’s had more than one experience of assault on a platform. At those times, he reported only feeling concern about the safety of his girlfriend he was with at the time.

↑ Observational Research: A male subway goer walking dangerously close to the platform edge.

That leaves us with two insights: (1) women modify their behavior in ways that offer more mental than physical protection, again even though they are less often victimized; and (2) men have less fears about their safety so many don’t take safety precautions…but then are found to be victims of more crimes.
Prototyping
Phase
I used the insights generated in my round of research to formulate three how might we (HMW) questions, which ultimately guided the development of my lo-fi and mid-fi prototypes:

Prototype 1
Meta insight:
Women’s fear of subway crime is more of a problem than crime itself.
HMW:
Make women feel more mentally secure when riding the subway?
Description:
A mobile feature that connects subway riders with other nearby riders, thereby providing a means for them to connect and request company should they ever feel unsafe. This feature would most likely be used at night, when subway ridership is low.
User Testing Findings:
  • People need to feel like they can trust the other people on this app
  • People said having a specialized app dedicated to subway safety felt more trustworthy than it being simply a feature in a pre-existing app
  • 67% of females prefer to crowdsource the help of another woman over a man


Prototype 2
Meta insight:
Women’s fear of subway crime is more of a problem than crime itself. (same as above)
HMW:
Make women feel more mentally secure when riding the subway? (same as above)
Description:
A navigation feature that shows users the optimal route to get somewhere based on calculated safeness. Safeness would primarily be a measure of how busy a station is.
User Testing Findings:
  • People report that knowing exactly how busy a station can be anxiety producing, especially if that number is low and they have no other alternative of getting where they need to go
  • People aren’t only anxious on the subway at night, but often on other parts of their commute as well (especially sidewalks)


Prototype 3
Meta insight:
Women modify their behaviors in ways that make themselves feel mentally safer, but not necessarily physically safer on the subway system.
HMW:
Revamp the subway system to alleviate the need for behavior modification? (So that women will get the mental peace of mind they crave, and men can continue to be unconcerned with safety, but in a safer environment.)
Description:
A rider ID card. Users of select cohors (e.g. women, youth) would have access to a mobile ID card that lets them ride on certain carriages at certain less safe times of day. Note that for this prototype idea, there would have to be an accompanying barcode-scanning technology implemented in those select subway carriages.
User Testing Findings:
  • People feel that riding in a segregated way has a bad connotation
  • People felt that barcode scanning has negative implications on the efficiency of ingress and ingress of subway cars
  • People feel that implementing barcode scanners is an expensive endeavor, and would likely be a cost that riders would have to cover


Prototype 4
Meta insight:
Subway crime and ridership have an inverse relationship, as do subway crime and police presence.
HMW:
How might we increase subway ridership to pre-pandemic levels?
Description:
An AR police (wo)man that would patrol subway cars and that you could interact with like you would a real police officer.
User Testing Findings:
  • People really don’t like the thought of being surveilled
  • People feel safer at the thought of immediate help, as opposed to the delayed help that cameras offer
  • People don’t want to walk up to a hologram and interact with it
  • People prefer more subtle ways of asking for help, like a discreet button
  • Increased police presence doesn’t make everyone feel safer

↑ Mid-fi prototyping for a crowdsourced safety and navigation app.

Based on user feedback, I tabled my third (mobile ID card) and fourth (AR police officer) ideas, and brought my first two ideas for a crowdsourced safety feature and a safest route feature into the mid-fi level. Further user testing revealed overwhelming preference for the crowdsourced safety feature, which was then scoped and prototyped as a hi-fi MVP.
